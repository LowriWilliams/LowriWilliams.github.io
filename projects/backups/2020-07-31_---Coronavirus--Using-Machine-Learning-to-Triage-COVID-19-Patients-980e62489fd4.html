INTRODUCTION

In almost 5 months, the current novel coronavirus (COVID-19) will have been in outbreak for a year. Whereas we’re still far from knowing how to fully tackle the virus, we’ve seen some pretty cool tech solutions towards social distancing (e.g. apps to order food), as well as those in the medical world (e.g. automatically detecting pneumonia-like indications in lung X-rays).

A few weeks ago, a mutual friend of ours approached us with a hypothesis. Dr Patrick Fafet, a medical doctor at the CHU in Montpellier, France, and his team had been triaging patients expressing COVID symptoms over the phone. They quickly realised, aside from the known symptoms of the virus, a possible indication of whether a patient suffered from COVID was the distinction in their coughs. With the sudden surge of patients calling and requesting to be tested or seen by a doctor, a solution towards supporting the triaging of possible COVID patients was needed.

Respiratory sounds are already known to have diagnostic properties. There is also some initial evidence that respiratory sounds, especially coughing, could be indicative of the virus. Until recently, such signals were usually collected during manual auscultation using a stethoscope. Research has now started to use digital technology to gather bodily sounds, and run automatic analysis on the data.

This post discusses our initial approach towards applying machine learning to analyse and classify COVID-positive and COVID-negative patient cough sounds. The data used in this experiment was collected from real patients using a phone triage system and was consented to be used to train the learning model. The data currently cannot be made publicly available.

DATA ANALYSIS

The initial dataset used to build the foundations of the model consisted of 251 .mp3 files and a .csv spreadsheet detailing their ID and the COVID test results which served as their labels.

The distributions of some of the patient details are shown in the following histograms. The data consisted of a significantly large number of negative COVID patients, as well as a larger number of female participants and participants who didn’t smoke. The dataset also included patients between the ages of 8 and 81, with a large population of patients being between 22 and 34 years old.

Dr Fafet noted, to the human ear, one of the main differences between COVID and non-COVID coughs is how unclear a patient’s inspiration is. In this case, we first analysed the differences in patient coughs, specifically the differences in the inhale of air after the cough.

When we talk about sound, we generally talk about a sequence of vibrations in varying pressure strengths. So to visualise sound means to visualise airwaves in some way. We can achieve this by converting the audio files into spectrograms. A spectrogram is a visual representation of the spectrum of frequencies of a signal as it varies with time.

In order to convert the data into spectrogram representations, we used LibROSA, an open-source Python package for music and audio analysis. The resulting representation can be shown as a 2D image and is called a Short-Time Fourier Transform (STFT). As humans perceive sound on a logarithmic scale, we convert the STFT to the Mel Scale, the result of the non-linear transformation of the frequency scale. This Mel Scale is constructed such that sounds of equal distance from each other on the Mel Scale also “sound” to humans as they are equal in distance from one another. FEATURE SELECTION

Dataset preprocessing, feature extraction and feature engineering are the steps taken to extract information from the underlying data. In the context of machine learning, this information should be useful for predicting the class of a sample. In audio analysis, this process is largely based on finding components of an audio signal that can help distinguish it from other signals.

We analysed the sounds of coughing using frequency-based variables. These fingerprints capture universal audio information and encode nuances or patterns that cannot be easily handcrafted. Joel Jogy’s post named ‘How I Understood: What features to consider while training audio files?’ provides an overview of some of the most important features that may be needed to build a model for an audio classification task and includes how to extract them using LibROSA.

Some of the features extracted from the cough samples were:

  • Chroma-based features which calculates how much of each chromatic pitch class (C, C♯, D, D♯, E, F, F♯, G, G♯, A, A♯, B) exists in the signal.
  • Mel-Frequency Cepstral Coefficients (MFCCs). Pratheeksha Nair’s post named ‘The dummy’s guide to MFCC’ notes that any sound generated by a human is determined by the shape of their vocal tract (including the tongue, teeth, etc). If this shape can be determined correctly, any sound produced can be accurately represented.
  • Zero-crossing rate measures the number of times the amplitude of a signal passes through a value of zero in a given time frame.
  • Spectral Centroid is an indicator of the “brightness” of a given sound, representing the spectral centre of gravity. If you were to take the spectrum, make a wooden block out of it and try to balance it on your finger (across the X-axis), the spectral centroid would be the frequency that your finger “touches” when it successfully balances.
  • Spectral Roll-Off is the frequency below which is contained 99% of the energy of the spectrum.
  • The Root Mean Squared (RMS) of the waveform that corresponds to its loudness.

NEURAL NETWORK

Gideon Mendels’ post named ‘How to apply machine learning and deep learning methods to audio analysis’ notes how to covert the audio features into NumPy arrays, how to encode the classification labels, and how to split the data into training and testing sets. CONCLUSION

So, what did we learn from this analysis?

These results only scratch the surface of the potential of this type of data; while our results are a positive sign, they are not yet robust enough to constitute a standalone screening tool. Detecting slight differences in cough sounds accurately is a really challenging task.

However, the model was released into the wild and integrated as part of the phone recording software. This was to ensure that more data from tested patients was collected. The model is systematically re-trained with the newly collected data and the parameters are tuned to achieve as high accuracy as possible. The responder is given an accuracy score of how likely the caller has COVID based on their cough. With this information, as well as other symptoms and their conversation with the patient, the responder is able to determine whether the patient needs to be seen and/or tested.

As part of improving the model, our next step is to investigate whether the spectrograms of cough sounds can also be incorporated. Elements of sound classifications can also be tackled as a traditional image classification task using Convolutional Neural Networks which may achieve higher accuracy.
